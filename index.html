Chaos Theory

A Dramatic Demonstration

scroll

Amongst tall marble columns,
mosaic floors,
and breathtaking paintings,
a brass sphere suspended on a wire
takes center stage.


It sways back and forth,
back and forth,
back and forth.

A pendulum.


This one, a Foucault pendulum at the Pantheon of Paris,
was built to show how the Earth rotates.

The swing stays straight, as the Earth turns beneath it.

Even now, nearly 175 years since its invention,
people still gather around and watch.


Something about its repetitive sway is hypnotic.


Consistent.


Reliable.


Trustworthiness is why the pendulum became such an integral part of our world.
Its stable rhythm was the heartbeat of every metronome, church tower, and beloved grandfather clock.

[ye single pendulum]

Watch it sway.

Pendulums aren't just predictable in practice, but also in principle: Every position leads to exactly one future, something so simple to predict that the math can be done on paper, and simulated on computers.

This is what Isaac Newton saw in the pendulum's swing. Physical proof that nature has clean, mathematical rules, and obeys them. If you have a pendulum's position and velocity, you can predict where it could be, at any moment in the future, forever.

Within a pendulum, the universe seems to keep its promises.


...That is, until we add another arm.

[ye double pendulum]

A Double Pendulum.

Just one more arm added before the first.

This one's movements are familiar,
swaying back and forth, just like many pendulums do.
It's clear that Newton's laws hold firm.

But now, things can get very strange.

[ye double pendulum]

This is the same double pendulum,
with its starting position set a little higher.

From this simple adjustment,
it now unpredictably jumps, loops, and
zig-zags across the screen.

It's future trajectory feels impossible to grasp.
The path it takes can feel random,
but true randomness can't be predicted.

The simulated pendulum is proof of this:
Every move it takes is still derived from Newton's equations,
meaning nothing is being invented, or left to chance.

The math is perfect.
The system is not.

And this system has a name.

A chaotic system.

Chaotic systems like double pendulums can exhibit
complex, unpredictable motion.

Predicting a chaotic double pendulum's first swing can be easy,
but it's next 10, 20, 30 swings can become unfathomable.

So where does this "chaos" come from?

The "clock" below turns at 0.015 radians per second,
and takes around 7 minutes to complete a full circle.

If the clock arm was 10cm long,
it would be turning at the pace of a garden snail.

Trying to "push" a pendulum at a garden snail's pace would mean
barely brushing your finger against it.

So will a "push" this slow change anything at all?
The results speak for themselves.

Below, there are two double pendulums
with the exact same starting position.

The white arm begins perfectly still,
while the blue arm begins with the
0.015 radians per second "push".

[two double pendulums!]

For the first 10 seconds, they appear nearly identical.
The line they draw doesn't diverge.
But then, something shifts,
and the pendulums start to stray.
...Soon enough, neither pendulum bears any semblance to the other,
with each following the beat of their own drum.

Same rules, same gravity, same starting height.
Nothing truly changed other than the tiny, nearly imperceptible difference in their starting velocity.

This is what scientists call

Sensitivity to Initial Conditions

Neither pendulum on its own is random: If you freeze time, each pendulum's immediate next position can be calculated. But to predict one's course of direction forever, you'll need to know the pendulum's starting position with perfect, infinite precision.

Thing being, nothing in the real world allows that.

Edward Lorenz, 1961 — two weather patterns from nearly identical starting points, diverging into utterly different futures.

This is what Edward Lorenz, an American Mathematician and Meteorologist, observed in his work while at MIT.

Like Newton and the pendulum, Lorenz pursued how to predict the future using physics and math. In his case: the weather.
In an effort to find a way to forecast the future, he built a simulaton using twelve differential equations on a primitive computer. It ran continuously, producing patterns that uncannily resembled real, believable weather.

But in January 1961, Lorenz took a shortcut. Instead of starting over to examine an earlier run, he started midway through, manually typing in the numbers himself for the machine's initial conditions from an earlier printout.

Instead of producing a copy of the earlier run, the new run showed dramatically different results, so much so that within a few simulated "months", neither run seemed to resemble the other.

Turns out, six decimal places were stored in the original computer's memory:

0.506127



In the printout, only three decimals appeared, rounded off to save space, which Lorenz entered.

0.506



Lorenz entered the second, assuming a difference in the ten-thousandths couldn't result in any significant consequences.

A mistake in the ten-thousandths.
For scale, this is just 7 drops of water in a full gallon.
The height of a young child compared to Mount Everest.
8.6 seconds of a 24 hour day.

This phenomenon, which Lorenz famously dubbed "The Butterfly Effect", would result in a new branch of physics and mathematics, known as

Where small, tiny, almost imperceptible changes can lead to drastically different and unpredictable consequences.

So, is Newton wrong?

No, his rules hold.

Chaos Theory simply revealed just how fragile those rules are.
